Manners of Articulation

×
  • Stops: consonants in which the airflow is completely stopped or blocked at the place of articulation by the tongue, lips, or vocal folds.
  • Fricatives: consonants in which air is forced through a narrow opening, causing friction.
  • Affricates: sequences of a stop immediately followed by a fricative, but behaving as a single sound:
    for example, t̓ᶿ is sort of like t̓ + θ, c is sort of like t + s, č is sort of like t + š.
    or
    tth’ is sort of like t’ + th, ts is sort of like t + s, tsh is sort of like t + sh.
  • Resonant: consonants involving less obstruction than a fricative: nasals (m, n), liquid (l), glides (y, w).

Glottalized Consonants

×
Glottalized consonants are produced with glottal articulation (a “catch in the throat”) as well as oral articulation, written with an apostrophe < ̓> on top of or beside the sound, for example m̓ or m’ = m with a glottal catch. Glottalized resonants typically have the catch in the after the resonant, but maybe have in before when they follow a stressed, non-long vowel. Glottalized stops use the catch in the throat a little bit differently, and have it during the stop to help increase the air pressure in the mouth and give the stop a “poppy” quality.

Labialized Consonants

×
Labialized consonants are produced with rounded lips, written with a w or superscript w <ʷ> after the consonant’s letter, for example qʷ ow qw = q with rounded lips. Note that while the w or ʷ is written after the letter, you should round your lips throughout the sound.

About

×

This tool was inspired by the Interactive Sagittal Section by Daniel Currie Hall. The division of linguistic features adheres to features in hul’q’umi’num’ and hən̓q̓əmin̓əm̓, as laid out on xwulmuhw.

The mid-sagittal image is a partial replication of that used by Currie Hall [The general structure of this sagittal section is based on diagrams in The sounds of language: An introduction to phonetics, by Henry Rogers].
Illustrations for voicing, glottalization, and some manners were designed by Sonya Bird and Chloë Farr.
Orthography follows that used by the hul’q’umi’num’ Language and Culture Society.
